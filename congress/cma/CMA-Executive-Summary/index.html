    The Congressional Modernization Act (CMA)¶

    Executive Summary¶

    Published January 2026

    Modernizing Congressional Operations and Electoral Administration

    The Institutional Challenge¶

    Congress operates with constitutional machinery designed for 4 million Americans now serving 335 million -- an 84-fold population increase with no corresponding institutional adaptation. Representatives serve 760,000 constituents each, twenty-five times the founding ratio and six to seven times larger than peer democracies. States maintain fifty different ballot access regimes creating litigation risk and administrative burden. Winner-take-all single-member districts enable geometry-driven representation distortions regardless of which party controls redistricting.

    These aren't moral failures -- they're operational deficiencies in constitutional infrastructure that reduce congressional capacity, increase dysfunction risk, and create unnecessary vulnerability to demographic and political shocks.

    The Solution: Simultaneous Integrated Reform¶

    CMA updates electoral and legislative infrastructure through an integrated package of evidence-based reforms. Members receive immediate capacity improvements upon enactment. Electoral reforms launch together at the First CMA Election as a unified system -- producing "the new Congress" rather than "more of the old Congress." Cube root compliance is achieved within approximately 14 years -- three presidential terms.

    The core insight: Expanding the House without reforming the electoral system simply creates more gerrymandered districts and amplifies existing dysfunction. CMA ensures STAR voting, multi-member districts, and House expansion debut together as an integrated operating system for American democracy.

    Core Framework¶

    Tier 1: Immediate Capacity Enhancements (upon enactment)¶

    • Phased compensation increases and automatic COLA adjustments
    • Professional development resources (5% of salary annually)
    • Enhanced staff budgets reducing talent drain to executive agencies
    • Improved pension structures reducing post-Congress lobbying dependency
    • Streamlined Senate appointment authority preventing administrative paralysis

    Tier 2: Integrated Electoral Reform Launch (First CMA Election)¶

    • Standardized ballot access reducing state-by-state litigation
    • Federal party recognition with Joint Endorsement Lists
    • House expansion to 510 members (first step toward cube root)
    • Multi-member districts with STAR-PR begin phasing in (25%)
    • STAR voting implemented for all federal elections

    Tier 3: Competency Transparency (Second CMA Election)¶

    • FCAO examinations become operational
    • Scores appear on ballots for voter information
    • Non-qualifying structure preserved -- no minimum score required

    Why This Approach Works¶

    1. Immediate Benefits, Integrated Reform¶

    All compensation and capacity improvements take effect immediately upon enactment. Electoral changes launch together at the First CMA Election as a unified system -- not piecemeal over decades.

    Current members receive full benefits regardless of when they face new electoral systems. The structure doesn't ask you to vote for distant uncertainty -- it provides present certainty with near-term modernization that members who vote for CMA will see during their careers.

    2. Risk Mitigation Through Standardization¶

    Ballot Access Standardization (Title I)

    • Eliminates fifty-state patchwork creating litigation exposure
    • Reduces administrative burden on state election officials
    • Provides predictable framework preventing ballot access disputes
    • 0.5% signature threshold (capped at 5,000 statewide) balances accessibility with legitimacy

    Federal Party Recognition Standards (Title II)

    • Establishes clear criteria preventing state-by-state arbitrary decisions
    • Reduces litigation over party status disputes
    • Provides transparency through Joint Endorsement Lists showing coalition structures

    3. Institutional Capacity Restoration¶

    House Expansion (Title III)

    • Initial expansion to 510 members at First CMA Election
    • Biennial +35 seat increases until cube root compliance (~720 members)
    • Cube root achieved within ~14 years -- three presidential terms
    • No member loses their seat -- expansion only, no displacement
    • Reduces constituent load from 760,000:1 to approximately 465,000:1
    • Improves district staff capacity for constituent services
    • Dilutes individual member obstruction power toward founders' design
    • Less expensive than expanding executive branch capacity equivalently

    Enhanced Compensation (Title VI)

    • Phased salary increases over five years:
    • House: $174,000 (current) rising to $250,000 (Year 5)
    • Senate: $174,000 (current) rising to $300,000 (Year 5)
    • The Senate differential reflects distinct constitutional responsibilities (advice and consent, statewide constituencies, six-year terms)
    • Automatic COLA tied to Employment Cost Index begins after Year 5
    • Enhanced pension accrual (2.5% vs. 1.7%)
    • Professional development allowance (5% of base salary: $12,500 House / $15,000 Senate)
    • Improved staff budgets through enhanced MRA (35% increase upon initial expansion)

    These aren't "raises" -- they're institutional capability improvements reducing dependence on fundraising, post-Congress lobbying, and executive branch salary competition.

    4. American-Developed Voting Methods¶

    STAR Voting (Score Then Automatic Runoff)

    • American innovation, not imported foreign model
    • Eliminates vote-splitting without complex elimination rounds
    • Administratively simpler than ranked-choice voting systems
    • Ensures winners have both high overall support AND majority preference
    • Reduces manipulation opportunities regardless of which party controls process
    • Consistent voter experience across all federal elections

    Unified Election Structure

    • Single general elections for all federal offices -- no state-administered primaries
    • All qualified candidates appear directly on November ballot
    • STAR voting ensures winners have broad support without preliminary filtering
    • Significant cost savings: one election instead of two
    • Higher turnout (general elections consistently outperform primaries)
    • Consistent voter experience regardless of district type or state of residence
    • Voters choose individual candidates, not party lists
    • Party autonomy preserved: parties may conduct internal nomination processes at their own expense

    Multi-Member Districts with Proportional Representation

    • Mathematically eliminates geometry-driven representation distortions
    • Makes redistricting manipulation ineffective for any party
    • Historically American (used 1789-1967) -- reinstating proven method
    • Phases in gradually: 25% to 50% to 75% to 90% to 100% over five election cycles

    Administrative Efficiency Gains:

    • States save millions annually on duplicate primary infrastructure
    • Voters make one trip to polls for federal elections instead of two
    • Election administrators handle one federal election cycle instead of two

    5. Competency Transparency Without Barriers¶

    FCAO Examinations (Title V)

    • Non-qualifying information mechanism -- no minimum score required
    • Candidates scoring 0% can still run and serve if voters choose them
    • Voters remain the sole gatekeepers -- FCAO provides data, not authority
    • Multi-branch appointment structure (President, Chief Justice, House Speaker, Senate Leader) prevents partisan capture
    • Zero power to block any candidate from ballot or service
    • Comparable to nutritional labels -- information for consumer choice, not regulatory barrier
    • Launches at Second CMA Election with adequate development runway

    Constitutional Authority¶

    Elections Clause (Article I, Section 4) provides plenary congressional power over federal election administration. Supreme Court precedent confirms authority over:

    • Voting methods (Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission)
    • Ballot access standards (U.S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton)
    • Manner of holding elections (Ex parte Yarbrough)

    House Size Adjustment requires only ordinary legislation -- no constitutional amendment. Congress adjusted House size 13 times between 1789 and 1913. The 1929 cap was statutory choice, not constitutional mandate.

    Multi-Member Districts have 178 years of American precedent and explicit Supreme Court validation (Whitcomb v. Chavis, White v. Regester).

    Implementation Safeguards¶

    Tiered Effective Dates¶

    Tier 1 -- Immediate upon enactment:

    • Compensation and capacity improvements (Title VI)
    • Enforcement authority (Title VII)
    • Administrative framework (Title VIII)

    Tier 2 -- First CMA Election (second general election after enactment, 18-month minimum):

    • Ballot access standards (Title I)
    • Party recognition framework (Title II)
    • House expansion to 510 (Title III)
    • Multi-member districts begin at 25% (Title IV)
    • STAR voting for all federal elections

    Tier 3 -- Second CMA Election (first election after First CMA Election):

    • FCAO examinations operational (Title V)

    Ongoing -- Biennial increases:

    • +35 seats per Congress until cube root compliance (~14 years total)

    Multiple Checkpoints¶

    Each expansion provides assessment opportunity before proceeding. If early implementation reveals problems, adjustments occur before further expansion.

    Severability Protection¶

    If courts strike down any provision, others continue operating. Compensation provisions that take effect remain protected even if electoral reforms are challenged.

    Supermajority Repeal Protection¶

    Once compensation mechanisms take effect, reducing or eliminating them requires three-fifths vote in both chambers -- preventing simple-majority backlash.

    Proven Methods, American Context¶

    These reforms draw on:

    Historical American Precedent: Multi-member districts used 1789-1967; Congress adjusted House size 13 times through 1913

    Constitutional Authority: Elections Clause grants plenary power; Supreme Court precedent validates multi-member districts (Whitcomb v. Chavis, White v. Regester)

    Operational Testing: Pilot implementations in select states provide proof-of-concept before nationwide expansion

    The question isn't whether these methods work -- American history and constitutional precedent demonstrate they do. The question is whether congressional infrastructure should continue operating with machinery that hasn't been updated since population tripled.

    What This Is Not¶

    Not a power grab by either party: Universal benefits regardless of party affiliation, district safety, or seniority. Both caucuses gain capacity improvements. Geometry-proof districts protect both parties from manipulation.

    Not deferred to distant future: ~14-year implementation means members who vote for CMA see results during their careers. Voters experience tangible improvements within a political generation. No more 25-year promises that outlive political accountability.

    Not swamp expansion: House growth is cheaper than equivalent executive branch capacity expansion. Dilutes rather than concentrates individual member power. Restores constitutional representation ratios without increasing leadership authority.

    Not untested innovation: Every reform draws on American historical precedent (multi-member districts 1789-1967; Congress adjusted House size 13 times through 1913).

    Not constitutional overreach: Operates within clear Elections Clause authority and congressional house-sizing powers validated by Supreme Court precedent.

    Not piecemeal tinkering: Comprehensive operational modernization with integrated reforms launching together -- producing "the new Congress" rather than amplifying existing dysfunction.

    Bottom Line¶

    CMA modernizes congressional operations and electoral administration using methods with proven track records in American history and tested in U.S. jurisdictions. It provides immediate capacity improvements through enhanced compensation and resources while launching integrated electoral reforms at the First CMA Election.

    Reform on a human timescale: Cube root compliance within ~14 years -- three presidential terms. Members who vote for CMA will see results. Voters will experience tangible improvements in representation quality.

    Simultaneous integrated launch: STAR voting, multi-member districts, and House expansion debut together, ensuring the new seats are filled through the new system -- not replicated dysfunction.

    Immediate member benefits: Compensation and capacity improvements take effect upon enactment, honoring the Grand Bargain that makes passage politically viable.

    Current constitutional machinery was designed for 4 million Americans. We now have 335 million. The question isn't whether to modernize -- it's whether to modernize systematically with tested methods and extensive safeguards, or continue with ad-hoc patches that don't address root operational deficiencies.

    CMA chooses systematic modernization with:

    • Immediate capacity improvements for all members
    • Simultaneous integrated reform launch at First CMA Election
    • Reform timeline delivering results within a political generation
    • Constitutional defensibility under Elections Clause authority
    • American historical precedent rather than imported models
