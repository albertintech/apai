This document is a companion to Proportional STAR — The Basics. That article walks through a simplified three-seat election and introduces the key ideas — selection, accounting, the two windows, the gauge. This article uses a larger, more politically complex election and shows every calculation. If you haven't read the companion first, start there. If you have, this is where we open the hood.

The formula is Allocated Score. Each round, it does two things at two windows: Window 1 selects the candidate with the most weighted support and awards the seat. Window 2 processes the charge — the quota is deducted from the voters who backed the winner, starting with the strongest supporters. The math below shows exactly how that charge is calculated, tier by tier, every round.

The Race Preview

District 7 is a competitive multimember district electing five representatives. Democrats are the largest bloc and are expected to win at least two of the five seats — the question is whether they can take a third. Republicans should secure one seat through Chen, their endorsed standard-bearer, who also carries the Forward Party endorsement — but Walsh, running without any party endorsement and drawing support only from the district's far-right voters, faces long odds. The real drama is in the middle and on the left flank. Olson, an Independent with endorsements from both Forward and Green, is drawing from two different voter pools. Patel, the Green Party's own candidate, is a wild card: Green endorsed Olson as well, so the question is whether Green voters will have enough influence left after supporting Olson to carry their own candidate. Analysts expect the major parties to take their seats. It's the remaining seats that are up for grabs.

What the Formula Never Needed to Know

The Allocated Score formula counted weighted support, selected the leader, charged the quota, and repeated — five times, blind to everything except the scores on the ballots. The groups that formed around each winner were not declared or registered. They emerged from the scores. The proportionality of the outcome was not determined by a thumb on the scale or a backroom deal. It was a consequence of the arithmetic. Equal price, equal treatment, repeated until every seat is filled. That's how Proportional STAR counts every vote and makes every vote count.

Allocated Score. The formula powering Proportional STAR. Selects candidates, charges the quota tier by tier, makes change, repeats.
Selection (Window 1). The candidate with the highest weighted total is awarded the seat. Most support wins.
Accounting (Window 2). The quota is charged against the winner's supporters, starting from the highest-score tier. The cusp is where the charge stops.
Quota. Total ballots ÷ seats. The price of one seat — charged after selection, never checked before.
Cusp tier. The score tier where the fill line lands during accounting. All voters in the cusp share the remaining cost equally.
Ballot weight (gauge). Remaining influence. Starts at 1.00. Drops when your candidate is selected and the cost is processed. At 0.00, fully allocated.
Multi-tier fill. When the top tier doesn't cover the quota alone, it's fully consumed and the next tier becomes the cusp.
Coalition endorsement. A party endorsing another party's candidate. Visible on the ballot. Drives cross-scoring that can be decisive.
