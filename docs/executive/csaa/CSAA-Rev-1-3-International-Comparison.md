# Civil Service Accountability Act

## International Comparative Analysis

### Published February 2026

Based on Rev 1.3 of the Civil Service Accountability Act

---

## Overview

The United States is not the first democracy to grapple with questions about professional civil service versus political appointments. Every advanced democracy has addressed these questions, and the empirical evidence from peer nations provides powerful validation for the CSAA approach. When we examine how other successful democracies structure their governments, a clear pattern emerges: professional civil services with limited political appointments consistently outperform systems with extensive politicization.

This document surveys the civil service structures of peer democracies, analyzes governance outcomes, and addresses the "American exceptionalism" objection to international comparison.

---

## The United States as Outlier

The United States federal government authorizes approximately 4,000 political appointments scattered throughout the executive branch -- though empirical data shows no modern administration has ever functionally staffed more than approximately 2,000-2,100 of those positions in the first year of a presidential term. Even at the operational level of approximately 2,000 filled positions, the United States maintains dramatically more political appointments than comparable peer democracies.

| Country | Political Appointments | Civil Servants | Ratio |
|---------|------------------------|----------------|-------|
| Singapore | ~20 | ~85,000 | 1:4,250 |
| Germany | ~50 | ~500,000 | 1:10,000 |
| United Kingdom | ~100 | ~475,000 | 1:4,750 |
| Canada | ~150 | ~300,000 | 1:2,000 |
| France | ~200 | ~2,500,000 | 1:12,500 |
| **United States (authorized)** | **~4,000** | **~2,100,000** | **1:525** |
| **United States (functional)** | **~2,000** | **~2,100,000** | **1:1,050** |
| **United States (CSAA target)** | **~1,000** | **~2,100,000** | **1:2,100** |

Even when measured by the positions administrations actually fill, the United States operates with 10 to 15 times more political appointments per capita than most peer democracies. The CSAA's target of approximately 1,000 positions brings the United States closer to international norms while remaining above countries like the United Kingdom, Germany, and Singapore -- reflecting the genuine scale and complexity of the American federal government.

The critical insight is that the CSAA does not propose reducing from 4,000 to 1,000 -- it proposes formalizing the approximately 1,000 positions that administrations demonstrably need and can effectively manage, while eliminating the approximately 2,000-3,000 positions that exist on paper but remain chronically vacant or serve primarily as patronage rather than governance functions.

## Case Studies

### The United Kingdom: Westminster Model Excellence

The United Kingdom operates one of the world's most effective governments with approximately 100 political appointments serving a civil service of roughly 475,000 employees. This represents a ratio of one political appointment for every 4,750 civil servants -- roughly ten times fewer political appointments per capita than the United States even at operational staffing levels.

The British system operates through clear role differentiation. Cabinet ministers -- political appointees -- set policy direction, represent the government in Parliament, and make final decisions on major initiatives. Permanent Secretaries -- career civil servants who are the highest-ranking officials in each department -- manage operations, provide institutional continuity, execute ministerial directives, and offer candid professional advice.

When general elections change which party controls government, ministers change but Permanent Secretaries continue serving. This continuity enables smooth transitions between governments with radically different policy agendas. When Labour wins power after Conservative governments, the civil service implements Labour policies professionally. When Conservatives return to power, the same civil service implements Conservative policies with equal effectiveness.

British Permanent Secretaries typically serve 20 to 30 years in civil service before reaching these senior positions. They advance through competitive processes based on merit. They receive extensive professional development throughout their careers. They face accountability through parliamentary select committees, National Audit Office reviews, and public performance assessments. They cannot engage in political activities and must serve governments of any party with equal professionalism.

This system has endured for over a century, surviving wars, economic crises, imperial decline, and dramatic social change. It has served socialist Labour governments implementing nationalization programs and Conservative governments pursuing privatization with equal competence. The British civil service is widely regarded as one of the world's most professional and effective bureaucracies.

Critics might argue that the United Kingdom is smaller and less complex than the United States, making direct comparison inappropriate. This objection fails on several grounds. First, the UK manages genuinely complex challenges: universal healthcare through the National Health Service, comprehensive social welfare systems, sophisticated intelligence and security operations, and international relationships spanning its Commonwealth legacy. Second, the principle scales -- larger governments need more professional continuity, not less. Third, other large federal systems demonstrate similar patterns.

### Germany: Federal Excellence Through Professional Service

Germany, with a federal system more comparable to the United States, operates with approximately 50 political appointments in the federal government. State Secretaries (Staatssekretare) -- the equivalent of American Deputy Secretaries -- are career civil servants (Beamte) with permanent status, extensive experience, and deep institutional knowledge.

German ministers -- the political appointees -- set policy within their ministerial portfolios. State Secretaries manage departmental operations, coordinate with other ministries, maintain institutional memory, and execute ministerial directives professionally. This division of labor has enabled German governments to function effectively through multiple coalition governments involving different party configurations.

The German civil service (Offentlicher Dienst) operates under constitutionally protected principles emphasizing merit-based selection, political neutrality, lifetime employment for core positions, and professional standards. Civil servants swear oaths to uphold the Basic Law (Germany's constitution) rather than to serve any particular government or party. This constitutional framework creates legitimate professional authority independent of partisan politics.

Germany's civil service structure survived the transition from the Weimar Republic through Nazi dictatorship and postwar reconstruction, then successfully integrated former East German civil servants after reunification. Throughout these massive transitions, professional civil service provided stability and institutional continuity. Contemporary Germany ranks consistently among the world's best-governed countries, with high scores on government effectiveness, low corruption, efficient public services, and strong institutional quality.

### Canada: Westminster Principles in North American Context

Canada provides perhaps the most relevant comparison for the United States -- a North American federal system with Westminster-style professional civil service. Canada operates with approximately 150 political appointments serving a civil service of roughly 300,000 employees, yielding a ratio of one political appointment per 2,000 civil servants.

Canadian Deputy Ministers are career civil servants providing institutional continuity across government changes. Ministers -- the political appointees -- set policy direction and make final decisions. Deputy Ministers manage operations, provide professional advice, maintain institutional knowledge, and execute ministerial directives regardless of which party holds power.

This system has enabled Canadian governments to implement dramatically different policy agendas across party changes. Liberal governments pursuing activist social policies and Conservative governments emphasizing fiscal restraint both rely on the same professional civil service for effective implementation. The civil service's professional competence enables policy effectiveness regardless of political direction.

Canada ranks consistently higher than the United States on measures of government effectiveness, corruption perception, and institutional quality despite having far fewer political appointments. This superior performance cannot be attributed to simpler governance challenges -- Canada manages universal healthcare, bilingual federal operations, federal-provincial relations, resource-based economic challenges, and vast geographic diversity. Canadian performance demonstrates that professional civil service enhances rather than impedes effective democratic governance.

### Key Finding

None of these countries experience "deep state" problems. None suffer from conspiracy theories about unaccountable bureaucrats undermining democratic control. Their professional civil services enable effective governance regardless of which party holds power. They demonstrate empirically that professional civil service and democratic accountability are not only compatible but mutually reinforcing -- professional competence enables democratic will to be implemented effectively.

## Addressing the "American Exceptionalism" Objection

Some defenders of the current American system invoke "American exceptionalism" to argue that the United States requires more political appointments than other democracies. This argument deserves serious examination and fails on multiple grounds.

**First, the exceptionalism argument reverses the actual pattern.** If American democracy is exceptional, it should demonstrate exceptional performance, not inferior performance relative to peers. The empirical evidence shows the opposite: the United States ranks below peer democracies on government effectiveness and corruption measures despite claiming exceptional status.

**Second, American governmental challenges are not uniquely complex compared to peer democracies.** Canada manages bilingual federal operations, universal healthcare, and vast geographic diversity. Germany manages federal systems with strong state governments and complex social insurance programs. The United Kingdom manages sophisticated intelligence operations and complex social welfare systems. Singapore manages remarkable ethnic diversity and complex economic regulation. These countries face genuine complexity yet govern more effectively with professional civil services.

**Third, the American constitutional system actually requires more institutional continuity than parliamentary systems, not less.** Parliamentary systems enable rapid government formation and can remove failing governments through votes of no confidence. The American system of fixed terms and separated powers means failed administrations cannot be quickly replaced. This makes institutional continuity through professional civil service even more important for the United States than for parliamentary systems.

**Fourth, historical evidence contradicts the exceptionalism argument.** The United States operated with far fewer political appointments for most of its history. The explosion to 4,000 appointments is recent -- primarily post-1980. During periods when the United States had fewer political appointments, American government was generally more effective and more respected internationally. The expansion of political appointments correlates with declining government effectiveness, not improving performance.

**Fifth, the empirical data reveals that the 4,000-position system is itself a fiction.** No modern administration has ever functionally staffed more than approximately 2,000-2,100 positions. Average confirmation times have nearly tripled from 69 days under Reagan to 192 days under Biden. At the 100-day mark of any new administration, 85% of sub-cabinet positions remain empty. The system that exceptionalism defenders are protecting does not actually function as designed -- it produces chronic vacancies, acting officials with limited authority, and implementation gaps that harm governance regardless of which party holds power.

**The real American exceptionalism** should be America's capacity to learn from successful practices globally and implement them better. Professional civil service is not a foreign import contrary to American traditions -- it is a return to American best practices corrupted by recent politicization. The Pendleton Act of 1883 established merit-based civil service in response to patronage corruption. CSAA represents the next logical evolution of that reform tradition.

## Conclusion

The international evidence points to a clear conclusion: the United States is the outlier. American over-politicization represents the exception rather than the rule among advanced democracies. The dysfunction that generates "deep state" conspiracy theories results from America's unique system of excessive political appointments, not from some inherent problem with career civil service. Countries that maintain professional civil services avoid the dysfunction that characterizes contemporary American governance.

The CSAA's approach -- right-sizing political appointments to approximately 1,000 positions (1.25% of the senior policy and management workforce) while establishing professional standards, fixed terms, and transparent accountability -- brings American practice closer to international norms while remaining calibrated to the genuine scale and complexity of the American federal government. At a ratio of approximately 1:2,100, the United States under the CSAA would still maintain more political appointments per capita than the United Kingdom, Germany, or Singapore, but would close the gap from an order-of-magnitude outlier to a position within the range of comparable democracies.
