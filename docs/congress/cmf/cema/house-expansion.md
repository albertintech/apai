# House Expansion

## A Primer Series Article of the Congressional Elections Modernization Act (CEMA)

*Prepared by Albert Ramos for The American Policy Architecture Institute*

---

The size of the House of Representatives has not changed since 1929. For nearly a century, the chamber has held exactly 435 seats — a number set by a federal law passed when the country had fewer than half as many people as it has today. The population the House represents has more than tripled since that law took effect. The House itself has not grown by a single seat.

The Congressional Elections Modernization Act proposes to unfreeze that number. Under the Act's House Expansion framework, the House grows from 435 to roughly 720 seats — not to a figure picked by negotiation, but to a size set by a fixed mathematical rule tied to the nation's population, so that the chamber keeps pace with the country it represents.

The claim is straightforward: a House sized by a principled rule serves the public better than one fixed by a long-ago political accident. House Expansion is one of the changes that make up CEMA, supported by a national elections body the Act creates — [The Electoral Science Office](/congress/cmf/cema/the-electoral-science-office/) — to provide the technical and institutional backbone the change requires. This article makes the case for House Expansion to a reader coming to it fresh.

---

## A 1929 Number Governing a 2026 Country

Most people assume the number 435 means something. It does not. It is not written into the Constitution, and it reflects no principle about how many people one representative should serve. It is simply where the count stopped. A 1929 law capped the House at 435 members, and Congress has never revisited the cap. Every census since has redistributed those same 435 seats among the states; none has added to them.

That freeze was the exception, not the rule. For most of American history, Congress enlarged the House after nearly every census, keeping pace — imperfectly, but in the right direction — with a growing country. The chamber was meant to grow. The 1929 decision to stop was a product of its particular moment, and it was simply never undone. The population tripled; the House stayed put.

The cost of that freeze shows up in a single ratio. At the founding, each member of the House represented about 30,000 people. The framers treated that closeness as essential — a representative was meant to know the place they served and the people in it. Today, each member represents about 760,000 people. The relationship the design assumed has not merely stretched; it has changed in kind.

Consider what that number does to the basic work of representation. A district of 30,000 is a community a single person can come to know — its towns, its industries, the concerns that recur at its kitchen tables. A district of 760,000 cannot be known that way by anyone. It can only be managed: through staff, through mass mail, through data. A single office is expected to answer to a population larger than that of some entire nations, and the load leaves no other way to operate. The connection between a representative and a constituent becomes bureaucratic by necessity, not by anyone's choice. This is not a failure of any particular member or party. It is what happens when a body built for one scale is asked to operate at twenty-five times that scale without ever being resized.

That ratio is arithmetic, not opinion. Divide the population by 435 and the number comes out the same regardless of anyone's politics. Grow the chamber to roughly 720 seats and that same arithmetic runs the other way: the population each representative answers to falls from about 760,000 to about 465,000 — a reduction of roughly 39 percent. That is still far from the founding scale, but it is a meaningful step back toward a relationship a representative can actually carry. House Expansion begins from this fact and asks a simple question: if the chamber has not grown with the country, what would it take to make it do so — automatically, and on a rule rather than a whim?

---

## A Rule, Not a Number

CEMA's answer is to stop setting the size of the House by hand. Instead of a fixed figure that requires an act of political will to change — and that, predictably, never gets changed — the Act ties the size of the House to the size of the population through a fixed formula. The chamber grows because the country grows, on a schedule that runs on its own.

The formula is the cube root rule: the House is sized at roughly the cube root of the national population. For today's United States, that comes to about 692 seats, which the Act rounds to a legislatively convenient target of roughly 720. The rule is not arbitrary. Looking across the world's national legislatures, their sizes tend to cluster near the cube root of the populations they serve — a pattern observed in the data, not a convention that lawmakers anywhere set out to follow. CEMA takes that observed pattern and adopts it deliberately, as a fixed engineering rule rather than a one-time political bargain.

The point is not that the cube root is sacred, or that it can be proven the single best possible size. The point is narrower and sturdier: a principled, fixed rule beats a number frozen by accident. Absent some better-evidenced rule, the cube root puts the chamber squarely in the right range — large enough to bring district sizes back toward something a representative can actually serve, small enough to remain a working legislature rather than a crowd. It is an engineering choice, made once, that then maintains itself.

Expansion does not happen overnight, and it does not happen by upheaval. The Act phases it in over roughly fourteen years — about three presidential terms. The House makes an initial jump from 435 to 510 seats, then grows in steady increments each Congress until the cube root target is reached, after which it adjusts once per decade to track the population. This is deliberate, gradual, boring institutional maintenance — the legislative equivalent of finally resizing a building to fit the number of people who have to work in it.

One feature of the design matters more than any other for understanding what expansion is and is not. No sitting member loses a seat. Expansion works entirely by adding seats, which are distributed among the states as their populations warrant — the same process, called apportionment, that already divides the existing 435 seats among the states by population every ten years. Today, because the total is fixed, one state's gain is another's loss; expansion simply gives that process more seats to divide. Nobody is displaced, no incumbent is drawn out of a job, and no state loses representation it already has. The House gets bigger; it does not get rearranged at anyone's expense.

---

## Why the Size of the House Is the Lever

It would be easy to read House Expansion as a quality-of-life improvement — smaller districts, more accessible representatives, lighter caseloads — and stop there. Those benefits are real. But the size of the House does something more fundamental within CEMA's design: it sets how open the entire system can be.

Here is the mechanism, in plain terms. CEMA pairs expansion with [Percentage-Based Voting](/congress/cmf/cema/percentage-based-voting/), under which most representatives are elected from larger, multi-seat districts rather than one-winner districts. In a multi-seat district, the share of support a group needs to win one of the seats depends on how many seats the district has. A three-seat district takes roughly a third of the vote to win one seat; a five-seat district, roughly a fifth; a seven-seat district, roughly a seventh. More total seats in the House means more seats per district, and more seats per district means a smaller slice of support is enough to win one. The size of the House, in other words, is the dial that sets how low the bar to win representation can fall.

That makes expansion the lever behind the whole structure. Suppose someone looks at CEMA and worries that the threshold to earn a seat is still too high for smaller groups to clear. The remedy is not to tinker with the voting method or redraw any lines. The remedy is a bigger House. Grow the chamber, and every threshold in the system comes down automatically, because the arithmetic of seats-per-district does the work. The threshold mechanics themselves belong to [Percentage-Based Voting](/congress/cmf/cema/percentage-based-voting/); what House Expansion contributes is the realization that the size of the chamber is what governs all of them. Expand the House and you open the system; hold it small and you keep it closed.

This is what lifts House Expansion from administrative housekeeping to something central to the design. It is not just that a larger House represents people more closely. It is that the size of the House is the single setting that determines how openable American representation can be.

## Addressing Concerns

### "If this is about fair representation, why do some states still get left out?"

This is the most important question this change has to answer, and the honest answer begins by being clear about where the limit comes from. It does not come from CEMA. It comes from the map.

State borders are accidents of history — drawn over two centuries by colonial charters, surveyors' lines, treaties, and political bargains, almost never by any theory of what makes a sensible unit of representation. CEMA takes those borders as it finds them; it does not redraw them. But it has to live with a consequence of them. The Constitution guarantees every state at least one seat in the House, and seats are apportioned among the states by population. That means a state with very few people gets very few seats no matter how large the House grows — and a state with only one or two seats has nothing to be proportional across. One or two seats is one or two winners; there is no remainder to allocate.

The old saying applies exactly: you cannot make an omelet with a single egg. Proportional representation works by dividing a district's seats among its groups of voters according to their support. A state holding just one or two seats cannot do that — there are not enough seats to divide. So even at full expansion, some small states remain below the size where proportional representation becomes possible, and no achievable House can change that.

The numbers are specific. At roughly 720 seats, 42 of the 50 states — 84 percent — reach the three-seat minimum that proportional representation requires. That is up from 38 states today. Of the eight that fall short at 720, six hold exactly two seats, and two — Vermont and Wyoming — remain single-seat states. This is not a transition problem that more growth would eventually solve. It is a permanent, named category, and the scale of the gap shows why. Vermont is the closest of all the small states to gaining a second seat — and even Vermont would not reach one until the House passed 728 seats, just beyond the target. Wyoming is further still: bringing it up to the three seats that proportional representation requires would take a chamber of roughly 1,400 — nearly twice the size CEMA proposes. These are not near misses that a slightly larger House would resolve. They are structural, and they would persist at any House size the country could plausibly adopt. CEMA does not pretend it can reach these states. It names them as a defined exception and is honest that the design has a floor it cannot cross.

### "What would it take to get every state to proportional representation — 100 percent across the country?"

It is worth being plain about what reaching every state would actually require, because the answer clarifies why CEMA stops where it does. To bring 100 percent of states to proportional representation, you would have to stop drawing representation around state borders altogether — set those borders aside for this one purpose, while keeping them for everything else, and divide the whole country fresh into rational units of roughly equal population. Only then would every part of the country sit inside a district large enough to be proportional.

That is beyond what an act of Congress can do. Reorganizing representation around something other than state lines would require a constitutional amendment, not a statute — and even then it would be genuinely hard, because people are not spread evenly across the land. Some regions, the dense and irregular Northeast in particular, resist being carved into equal pieces no matter how the lines are drawn. So 100 percent is a ceiling set first by the Constitution and second by geography. CEMA goes as far toward that ceiling as a statute can reach, and is candid that the ceiling is there.

---

## What House Expansion Does and Does Not Reach

House Expansion changes the size of one chamber and nothing else. It grows the House of Representatives; it does not touch the Senate, where every state has two seats regardless of population, and it does not alter the Electoral College. It does not change any state's borders. It does not displace any sitting member. And it federalizes nothing beyond the size of the federal House, which the Constitution already commits to national resolution. The change is deliberately contained: one number, set by a rule instead of by accident.

---

## Conclusion

For nearly a hundred years, the House has been the same size while the country tripled around it. The result is a chamber whose members each answer to twenty-five times as many people as the design intended — a relationship that has become bureaucratic because the math left it no other way to be. House Expansion fixes the number at its root, replacing a figure frozen by political accident with a chamber that grows on a fixed rule as the country grows.

And it does more than restore closeness. The size of the House is the lever that sets how open American representation can be — the dial that, paired with [Percentage-Based Voting](/congress/cmf/cema/percentage-based-voting/), determines how small a group can be and still earn a seat. That is why this change is central rather than cosmetic. House Expansion works alongside the Act's other changes — [Percentage-Based Voting](/congress/cmf/cema/percentage-based-voting/), [Hands-Off Mapping](/congress/cmf/cema/hands-off-mapping/), and [Full-Field Elections](/congress/cmf/cema/full-field-elections/) — each supported by [The Electoral Science Office](/congress/cmf/cema/the-electoral-science-office/), to deliver a Congress sized to the nation it serves.

A House that grows with the country, on a rule rather than a frozen accident. That is the whole of it — and it is enough.
