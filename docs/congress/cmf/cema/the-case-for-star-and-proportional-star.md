# Congressional Elections Modernization Act

## The Case for STAR and Proportional STAR

---

Every other design choice in the Congressional Elections Modernization Act can be litigated on its own terms. The size of the House, the independence of the Electoral Science Office, the elimination of state-administered primaries -- each is a serious question, but none of them decides who actually wins. The voting method does. It is the single rule that converts what millions of voters want into the names that take office. Get it wrong and the rest of the architecture inherits the distortion; get it right and the rest of the architecture has something honest to build on.

The Act makes two choices at this most consequential point. In single-seat contests, it uses STAR Voting -- a method that finds the candidate most preferred by the most voters. In multi-seat contests of three or more seats, it uses Proportional STAR, which elects candidates in proportion to voter support. This paper argues that these are not least-bad compromises selected to avoid the failures of the alternatives. They are the right choices on the merits -- the methods that most faithfully record what a vote is supposed to mean, and the only methods coherent with the rest of the Act's design.

The case proceeds in four movements. First, a brief account of what the two methods do, because the argument depends on the mechanics being clear. Second, an honest engagement with the strongest objections, because a position worth holding survives its best critics rather than its weakest. Third, the affirmative case -- the positive reasons to prefer scored voting with an automatic runoff over the rank-based and single-mark alternatives. And fourth, the structural argument: why these particular methods are not merely good in isolation but required by the Act's larger architecture.

---

## What STAR and Proportional STAR Do

STAR stands for Score Then Automatic Runoff, and the name is the method. A voter scores each candidate from zero to five stars. The two candidates with the highest total scores advance to an automatic runoff, in which each ballot counts as a single vote for whichever finalist that voter scored higher. The candidate preferred on more ballots wins. The scoring round measures breadth and intensity of support across the whole field; the runoff round guarantees that the winner is the candidate the most voters actually prefer when the choice narrows to two. Neither round alone is sufficient -- a pure score election can elect a broadly inoffensive candidate whom most voters do not actually prefer to the runner-up, and a pure ranked election discards the intensity information that scores capture. STAR uses each round to discipline the weakness of the other.

Proportional STAR extends the same ballot to multi-seat districts using an Allocated Score method built on the Hare quota. Voters score candidates exactly as they do in a single-seat race. Seats are filled sequentially: the highest-scoring candidate is elected, the ballots that supported that candidate are proportionally exhausted by the quota's worth of voting power, and the process repeats on the remaining ballot weight until every seat is filled. The result is a body whose composition tracks the distribution of voter support -- without party lists, without closed slates, and without asking voters to do anything other than rate the individual human beings on the ballot. A voter who wants to know which member represents them has a clean answer: the one you gave five stars to.

Two features of this design deserve emphasis at the outset because the rest of the argument rests on them. The method is American in origin, developed by the Equal Vote Coalition rather than imported from a parliamentary tradition that solved different problems under different constraints. And the ballot is identical whether the district elects one member or seven -- the voter's task does not change with district magnitude, only the count behind the curtain does.

## The Strongest Objections

A position paper earns its conclusion by engaging the best arguments against it, not the most convenient ones. Three objections to scored voting are serious enough to require a serious answer.

### The ordinal supremacy argument

The most rigorous objection comes from social choice theory, and it runs roughly as follows: ranked (ordinal) ballots ask voters only to order candidates, which is something voters can do honestly without strategic calculation, whereas scored (cardinal) ballots ask voters to assign magnitudes, which invites strategic exaggeration and therefore corrupts the very information the method claims to collect. On this view, ordinal methods are epistemically cleaner because the data they gather is the data voters are willing to give honestly.

The objection proves less than it claims, and the reason is Gibbard-Satterthwaite. That theorem establishes that no deterministic, non-dictatorial voting method over three or more candidates can be strategy-proof. Strategic incentive is not a defect peculiar to cardinal methods; it is a structural feature of every method that aggregates more than two options, ordinal methods included. Ranked systems are strategically manipulable through burial, compromising, and a catalog of other documented maneuvers. The honest framing, then, is not "cardinal methods are manipulable and ordinal methods are not" -- that comparison is false -- but "every method is manipulable, so the relevant question is which method produces good outcomes under realistic, partly-strategic voting." The ordinal supremacy argument quietly holds cardinal methods to a standard no method meets, and excuses ordinal methods from the same test. Applied evenly, the standard does not discriminate between the families at all.

### Strategic compression and min-max voting

A more practical version of the same worry: voters will not use the full zero-to-five scale honestly. They will bullet-vote, giving five stars to their favorite and zero to everyone else, collapsing the scored ballot into something close to a single-mark ballot and discarding the intensity information that justifies the method.

This is a real behavior, and the honest concession is that some voters will do it. But the automatic runoff is precisely the structural answer. Even if a substantial share of voters min-max their scores, the runoff round recovers majority preference between the two finalists, because in that round every ballot resolves to a simple "which of these two did you score higher." A method that depended entirely on honest scoring would be fragile; STAR does not, because the runoff is a floor that holds even when the scoring round is gamed. The scoring round rewards voters who express gradations -- it gives them more influence over which two candidates advance -- but it does not punish those who do not, and it cannot be broken by those who refuse to.

### Cognitive burden

The third objection is that scoring is harder than marking a single box, that voters will be confused or fatigued, and that a more demanding ballot will depress participation or increase error among exactly the voters reform should empower.

The strongest answer is not theoretical but observed. STAR ballots have been used in binding and high-stakes contexts, and the usability evidence does not show the predicted collapse. The Python Software Foundation's Steering Council has used Bloc STAR for its 2025-2026 term elections -- a real organization choosing the method for real, consequential internal governance, not a laboratory exercise. Rating things on a five-point scale is among the most familiar tasks in modern life; it is how people evaluate restaurants, films, products, and drivers. The scored ballot asks voters to do something they already do reflexively, and the empirical record of actual STAR elections does not bear out the fear that they cannot.

## The Affirmative Case

The objections cleared, the positive argument is straightforward: scored voting with an automatic runoff records what voters actually think better than the alternatives, and it does so while resisting the pathologies that drive voters away from the ballot box.

**It captures intensity and breadth, not just order.** A ranked ballot knows that a voter prefers A to B, but not whether that preference is a chasm or a hair. A single-mark ballot knows even less. The scored ballot captures both how many candidates a voter can live with and how strongly they feel about each -- information that is real, that voters hold, and that every other method throws away. This is not a technical nicety. A representative body assembled from ballots that recorded the full texture of voter preference is a more accurate instrument of public will than one assembled from ballots that recorded only a ranking or a single name.

**It finds genuine majority support.** The automatic runoff guarantees that the winner of a single-seat race is the candidate a majority of voters prefer to the alternative who came closest. This is the property that plurality elections lack entirely -- under plurality, a candidate can win with the enthusiastic support of a third of voters and the active opposition of the other two-thirds -- and it is the property that makes a STAR result defensible to the losing side. The winner is not merely the candidate with the largest faction; the winner is the candidate the most voters actually preferred when it counted.

**It resists spoilers and vote-splitting.** Because voters can support multiple candidates without penalty, the entry of a similar candidate does not split a faction's support and hand victory to its opposition. The spoiler dynamic that deforms plurality elections -- and that disciplines voters into abandoning their sincere favorite for a "viable" choice -- loses its grip. Voters can rate their honest favorite five stars without fear that doing so wastes their vote, because the scoring round and the runoff together ensure that an honest five-star rating helps rather than harms the candidates a voter also finds acceptable.

**Proportional STAR delivers proportionality through individuals, not lists.** The multi-seat method produces a body whose makeup reflects the distribution of voter support, but it does so without the closed party lists that characterize most proportional systems and that sit poorly with American political culture. There is no slate, no party-determined ordering, no vote for an abstraction. Voters rate candidates, and proportionality emerges from those individual ratings. This preserves the candidate-centered, accountable, constituent-facing character of American representation while delivering the proportional outcomes that single-winner districts structurally cannot. It is proportional representation that an American voter can recognize as continuous with the way they already vote.

## The Structural Argument

Even granting everything above, a skeptic might ask why the Act needs these particular methods rather than any of the other reasonable reform options. The answer is that STAR and Proportional STAR are not interchangeable parts dropped into the design. They are the methods the rest of the architecture requires, and the requirement runs in two directions.

### Magnitude and method are a joint instrument

The most common objection from the proportional-representation literature is that some other method -- single transferable vote, in particular -- is "more proportional" than Proportional STAR. The objection misunderstands where proportionality comes from. District magnitude enables proportionality; the counting method delivers it within the space magnitude opens. A single-seat district cannot be proportional under any counting rule whatsoever, because there is exactly one winner and one winner cannot mirror a divided electorate. Proportionality is a property that becomes available only as the number of seats rises, and it becomes available to *any* reasonable method at the same magnitudes. The choice between Proportional STAR and a rival multi-seat method is therefore not a choice about how much proportionality the system delivers -- magnitude governs that -- but about how the system behaves on every other dimension at a given magnitude.

This is exactly why the Act institutes Proportional STAR only where it can do meaningful work: in districts of three or more seats. Below that threshold, proportionality is not on the table, and the question is moot; the Act uses single-winner STAR, the right tool for finding one majority-preferred winner. At three seats and above, magnitude opens the proportional space and Proportional STAR fills it. The two methods are not competitors within the Act; they are matched to the structural condition -- seat count -- that determines which question is even being asked. Proportionality is a joint product of magnitude and method, and the Act sets both deliberately rather than hoping a single method will paper over a magnitude problem.

### The architectural dependency: a method that does not need winnowing

The decisive structural argument is about what each method requires to function well, and it turns on a choice the Act makes elsewhere. The Act eliminates state-administered primaries; under the Unified General Election Structure, all qualified candidates appear directly on the general-election ballot. Primaries were the bottleneck, and the Act removes the bottleneck. But primaries also did hidden work for ranked methods: they winnowed crowded fields down to a manageable handful before the decisive election.

Single transferable vote depends on that winnowing. Its pathologies -- non-monotonicity, the premium it places on strategic lower-preference ordering, the sheer combinatorial difficulty of a long ranking -- scale with the size of the candidate field. STV performs acceptably when an external mechanism has already pruned the ballot to a small number of serious contenders, which is why the democracies that use it pair it with disciplined party nomination or pre-election winnowing. Remove the winnowing and STV degrades. A reform architecture that has just abolished primaries cannot then adopt a counting method that silently depends on primaries to function -- that would be incoherent, reintroducing through the back door the very bottleneck the front door eliminated.

Proportional STAR carries no such dependency. The scored ballot does not become harder to fill out as the field grows -- a voter rates as many or as few candidates as they wish, and ignoring a candidate is the natural default rather than a strategic error. The method's behavior does not degrade with field size, because it never asked the voter to produce a complete ordering in the first place. Proportional STAR is therefore not merely a good method that happens to be compatible with primary elimination; it is the method that primary elimination makes necessary. The Act removed the winnowing bottleneck, and only a method that does not need winnowing can stand in the space left behind.

This is the sense in which the choice is structural rather than aesthetic. One could imagine an alternate Act that kept primaries and used STV, and it might function. One could imagine the Act's primary elimination paired with a method that needs winnowing, and it would not. The Act made a coherent set of choices, and STAR and Proportional STAR are the voting-method half of that coherence.

---

## Conclusion

The methods a democracy uses to count votes are usually inherited, not chosen -- adopted long ago for reasons no one living remembers, and defended chiefly because changing them is hard. The Congressional Elections Modernization Act treats the question as one worth deciding on the merits, and the merits point clearly. STAR Voting records the full texture of what voters think, guarantees that single-seat winners are genuinely majority-preferred, and frees voters from the spoiler calculus that forces them to abandon their honest favorites. Proportional STAR extends that honesty to multi-seat districts, delivering proportional outcomes through individual candidates rather than party lists, in a form an American voter can recognize as continuous with how they already vote.

These are not compromises the Act tolerates. They are the methods that best honor what a vote is supposed to do, and -- given a design that has removed the primary bottleneck and matched method to magnitude -- the only methods coherent with everything else the Act builds. The voting method is the rule that decides who governs. The Act gets it right, and gets it right for reasons that survive the strongest objections to the contrary.
