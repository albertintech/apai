# Start Here

## An Introduction to the Congressional Elections Modernization Act (CEMA)

*Prepared by Albert Ramos for The American Policy Architecture Institute*

---

Most Americans sense that something has gone wrong with Congress. It feels distant, gridlocked, and strangely unresponsive — capable of campaigning endlessly but rarely of fixing anything. The usual explanations blame the people: the wrong party, bad politicians, a coarsened culture. Those explanations are popular because they are easy. They are also mostly beside the point.

The deeper trouble is structural. The machinery that turns votes into representation — how many seats the House has, how district lines get drawn, how ballots are counted, who is even allowed to appear on them — was built for a country that no longer exists, and much of it has not been seriously maintained in a century. When a system stops working, it is worth checking the wiring before blaming the people standing in the dark. The Congressional Elections Modernization Act is, at bottom, an attempt to check the wiring.

This is a guide to what the Act does and why. It is written for the citizen, not the specialist — no background in election law required. It introduces each of the Act's changes in turn and points you toward a short, plain-language primer on each one. Read this page first; then follow whichever changes you want to understand more deeply.

---

## The Idea Behind the Act

The name was chosen with care. This is a *modernization* act, not a reform crusade. The distinction matters, because the word points at the disposition behind the whole project: this is maintenance and upkeep, the kind any aging system needs, not a campaign to take power from one group and hand it to another.

The country does this routinely everywhere else. Bridges get inspected and reinforced. Power grids get upgraded as demand grows. Building codes get revised as we learn what fails. Nobody calls replacing century-old plumbing a radical act; it is just what responsible upkeep looks like. The rules governing congressional elections are infrastructure too — and they have gone without that kind of attention for a remarkably long time. Some of the load-bearing pieces date to the 1920s. One of them dates to the founding and was simply never revisited as the country grew around it.

Treating the problem as maintenance rather than moral failure changes what a solution looks like. You are not trying to defeat anyone. You are trying to bring the structure back up to the standard the country already expects of every other system it depends on. That is the spirit in which the Act is written, and it is the spirit in which this guide is meant to be read.

A second idea runs underneath the first: the changes work as a set. Each one addresses a real problem on its own, but they were designed to launch together, because they reinforce one another. A larger House makes fairer districts possible; fairer districts make a better counting method worth having; a better counting method makes an open ballot workable. You can understand them one at a time — that is what the primers are for — but they were built to function as a whole.

## What the Act Changes

The Act makes several distinct changes to how congressional elections work. Each has its own primer; what follows is the short version of each, and why it matters.

**House Expansion.** The House of Representatives has been frozen at 435 seats since a law passed in 1929 — a number set when the country had fewer than half as many people as it has today. Since then the population has more than tripled while the chamber has not added a single seat. The result is that each representative now answers to about 760,000 people, a relationship far too large to be anything but bureaucratic. House Expansion unfreezes that number, growing the House to roughly 720 seats on a fixed mathematical rule tied to the population, so the chamber finally grows with the country it serves. It also turns out to be the change that makes the others possible — the size of the House sets how open the whole system can be. → *See the House Expansion primer.*

**Hands-Off Mapping.** District lines today are drawn by the people who stand to gain or lose from where they go. The predictable result is gerrymandering: maps engineered to lock in outcomes before any votes are cast. Hands-Off Mapping removes human discretion from the process entirely — an algorithm draws the lines from census data by a fixed, public procedure, the same inputs always producing the same maps, with no thumb on the scale because there is no thumb at all. → *See the Hands-Off Mapping primer.*

**Percentage-Based Voting.** Most American elections still use the oldest and crudest counting rule: pick one candidate, most votes wins, everything else discarded. It splits votes, punishes voters for supporting who they actually prefer, and routinely hands seats to candidates most people voted against. Percentage-Based Voting replaces it. Voters score the candidates, the method finds the ones with the broadest genuine support, and in multi-seat districts, seats are awarded in proportion to how voters actually voted — so a group that wins a third of the support wins close to a third of the seats, rather than nothing at all. → *See the Percentage-Based Voting primer.*

**Full-Field Elections.** In much of the country, the election that truly decides who represents you is a low-turnout primary most voters skip — a contest where a small, committed slice of the electorate narrows the field before everyone else gets a say. Full-Field Elections put every qualified candidate on a single general-election ballot, where the whole electorate weighs the whole field at once. Parties still endorse whomever they like, and those endorsements appear right on the ballot — but they no longer filter candidates off it. → *See the Full-Field Elections primer.*

**The Electoral Science Office.** None of the above is worth much if no one can verify it works. Today no agency exists whose job is to test voting methods before they are used, measure them afterward, and publish the evidence so anyone can check it. The Electoral Science Office fills that gap. It is the change the others rely on to stay honest — the body that tests the methods, publishes the code and the data, and lets any citizen confirm the results rather than take them on faith. It does not run elections or count the official vote; it studies, proves, and publishes. → *See the Electoral Science Office primer.*

## How It Would Happen

A fair question about any change this substantial is whether it arrives as a shock. It does not. The Act is built to phase in deliberately, over years, not to flip overnight.

Some pieces take effect on enactment. The changes to how elections are actually run begin together at the first congressional election held under the new rules — far enough out to give states time to prepare. House Expansion then proceeds gradually, adding seats in modest, regular increments across roughly fourteen years until the rule's target is reached, after which the chamber simply adjusts once a decade to track the population. The pace is closer to routine institutional housekeeping than to upheaval — which is the point.

Crucially, no sitting representative is forced out by any of this. House Expansion works by adding seats, not redistributing existing ones away from anyone. The people asked to vote for these changes are not being asked to vote themselves out of a job.

## Is This Even Allowed?

It is reasonable to wonder whether Congress can do all this by ordinary law. For the most part, the answer is a straightforward yes, and it rests on authority the Constitution grants directly.

The Constitution gives Congress explicit power over the "times, places and manner" of congressional elections. That is broad authority, and it covers the machinery these changes touch: how votes are cast and counted, how districts are structured, how candidates reach the ballot, and how district lines are determined. The size of the House is a separate matter, and an even simpler one — it is set by statute, and Congress changed it many times in the country's first century and a half before freezing it in place. Raising it again asks for nothing the Constitution does not already permit.

In other words, this is not a project that depends on amending the Constitution or stretching it into new shapes. It works within authority that already exists and has simply gone unused. Where the Act reaches a genuine constitutional limit — and there are a few — it stops, names the limit honestly, and goes no further. The primers are candid about exactly where those limits fall.

## Where to Go From Here

This page is the doorway, not the house. Each change above has its own primer — a short, plain-language explanation you can read in one sitting, written to stand on its own. You do not have to read them in any particular order, and you do not have to read all of them. Start with whichever question brought you here.

If you want to understand why representation can feel so out of reach, start with **House Expansion** or **Percentage-Based Voting**. If gerrymandering is what bothers you, start with **Hands-Off Mapping**. If you have ever felt the real decision was made before you got to vote, start with **Full-Field Elections**. And if your first question is *how would I ever trust that any of this works* — the most important question of all — start with **The Electoral Science Office**.

The country maintains everything else it depends on. Its elections deserve the same care. These primers are an invitation to look at the wiring for yourself.
