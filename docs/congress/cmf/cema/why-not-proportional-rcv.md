# Why Not Proportional Ranked Choice Voting?

## A Position Paper of the Congressional Elections Modernization Act (CEMA)

*Prepared by Albert Ramos for The American Policy Architecture Institute*

---

Among people who want to reform American elections, proportional representation is close to consensus. The diagnosis is shared: winner-take-all, single-member districts distort representation, suppress competition, and entrench a two-party system that leaves millions of voters effectively unrepresented. The Congressional Elections Modernization Act (CEMA) accepts that diagnosis in full. The disagreement this paper takes up is not whether to adopt proportional representation. It is which proportional method to build the House on -- and that disagreement is narrower and more consequential than it first appears, because at any given district size, the competing proportional methods deliver comparable proportionality. The magnitude does that work. What separates the methods is everything else: how the ballot behaves, whether votes are discarded, whether the count can be gamed, and -- the question this paper places at the center -- what the method demands of the election held around it.

This paper makes a single claim and builds the rest on it: **the leading proportional reform, Proportional Ranked Choice Voting, can only function tolerably on a field that something else has already cut down to size -- and that hidden dependency on winnowing is exactly what CEMA's primary-free design cannot supply. Proportional STAR is the one proportional method that survives an unwinnowed field, which is why a reform that ends the primary requires it.** The pathologies catalogued against Proportional RCV in the pages that follow -- its non-monotonicity, its center squeeze, its ballot exhaustion, its surplus-transfer arbitrariness, its swelling ballot -- are not five independent complaints. They are five faces of one fact: the elimination engine degrades as the candidate field grows, and multi-member districts grow the field. A method that needs the field pre-trimmed needs an institution to do the trimming. In the American system that institution is the primary. CEMA abolishes it. The argument is that simple, and the rest of the paper is its consequences.

This is the same keystone logic that governs CEMA's single-winner choice, extended to the multi-seat case. The companion position paper, *The Case for STAR and Proportional STAR*, shows that ending the state-administered primary forces a single-winner method that needs no primary to function, and that STAR is that method. The claim here is the multi-seat counterpart: the same elimination engine that makes single-winner ranked choice depend on winnowing makes its multi-winner form, Proportional RCV, depend on it too -- and the dependency is worse in the multi-seat context, not better, because larger districts attract larger fields. The two papers rhyme because the architecture does.

The comparison throughout is to the method that actually competes for this job. Proportional RCV is the most prominent competing proportional method and the one proposed in the Fair Representation Act (H.R. 4632), introduced in July 2025 -- the only live federal proportional representation bill, and the fifth time its lead sponsor has carried it since 2017. FairVote, RepresentWomen, the League of Women Voters, the RCV Resource Center, and allied organizations have invested substantial institutional resources in positioning it as the natural next step for the American ranked-choice movement. CEMA takes that proposal seriously enough to measure itself against it, rather than against an imaginary method that fails at nothing.

The case that follows is not that Proportional RCV is worse than the status quo -- it is plainly better than winner-take-all. The case is narrower, and it turns on a single dependency. A ranked ballot works well only within a narrow band of candidates: enough to make a proportional result achievable, few enough that voters can actually rank them and the ballot stays manageable. The Fair Representation Act recognizes this and builds carefully to respect it -- a sign of serious design, not a flaw. But holding the field inside that band requires an institution upstream to manage how many candidates reach the voter, and in the American system that institution is the primary. CEMA abolishes the primary, because the harms primaries carry -- low and unrepresentative turnout, the exclusion of independents, the doubled trip to the polls, the public subsidy of private nomination contests -- are the very harms CEMA was built to end. Proportional STAR has no such band: it degrades gracefully as the field grows, so it needs no upstream winnowing, so it can live in a primary-free general election. That is why a reform that ends the primary requires it.

## What Proportional RCV Is

Proportional RCV is a multi-winner electoral system in which voters rank candidates in order of preference on a ranked ballot. Tabulation proceeds through a combination of quota-based election and sequential elimination.

A quota -- typically the Droop quota -- is calculated by dividing the total number of valid ballots by the number of seats plus one, then adding one. In a five-seat district with 100,000 voters, the quota is approximately 16,668. Any candidate whose first-choice vote total meets or exceeds the quota is declared elected. That candidate's surplus votes -- the ballots beyond the quota -- are then transferred to each ballot's next-ranked candidate, at a fractional transfer value reflecting the surplus. If no candidate meets the quota after surplus transfers, the candidate with the fewest votes is eliminated and their ballots are transferred to the next-ranked active candidate at full value. This process of electing, transferring surpluses, and eliminating continues until all seats are filled.

### A Note on Terminology

The vocabulary surrounding ranked methods is genuinely tangled, and precision matters before the analysis proceeds. Four terms recur in this debate:

- **Single Transferable Vote (STV)** is the formal, internationally recognized name for the multi-winner ranked method described above. It is the term used in the academic social choice literature and in the constitutions and statutes of the countries that have adopted it. When this paper engages the technical or historical record -- Irish and Maltese practice, the social choice criteria literature, named algorithmic variants -- STV is the precise referent, and the paper uses it accordingly.
- **Instant Runoff Voting (IRV)** is the single-winner ranked method. Mechanically, IRV is what STV becomes when there is only one seat to fill: the same fewest-first-preferences elimination engine, without surplus transfers. This relationship is central to the paper's argument and is examined in detail below.
- **Ranked Choice Voting (RCV)** is the American umbrella term for any system using a ranked ballot. In common usage it most often refers to single-winner IRV -- the version adopted in Alaska, Maine, New York City, and dozens of municipalities -- but the "ranked choice" label attaches to the ballot format, not to any specific tabulation rule.
- **Proportional Ranked Choice Voting (Proportional RCV)** is the term U.S. reform organizations have adopted for multi-winner STV. It extends the familiar "ranked choice voting" brand into the proportional space, signaling to American voters that the multi-winner system uses the same ranked ballot they may already know from single-winner RCV.

This paper adopts **Proportional RCV** as its primary term. The reason is straightforward: it is the term the advocacy community uses, and it is the term embedded in the only live federal proportional representation bill, the Fair Representation Act. Americans are this paper's audience, and the paper meets them in the vocabulary they will actually encounter in the public debate. STV is the name familiar to audiences abroad and to the academic literature; it is not the name under which this method is being marketed to the American electorate. The paper therefore leads with Proportional RCV while remaining free to use STV, IRV, and RCV wherever each is the more precise referent -- STV for the technical and comparative-historical record, IRV for the single-winner elimination engine, and RCV for the broader ranked-ballot family. Nothing in the terminology changes the underlying mechanics; the method proposed in the Fair Representation Act and the method analyzed here are the same.

### The IRV Relationship

The relationship between Proportional RCV and IRV is not analogical -- it is mechanical. Proportional RCV uses the same elimination algorithm as IRV: identify the candidate with the fewest first-choice votes, eliminate that candidate, and redistribute their ballots. When the method is applied to a single-winner election, it *is* IRV. The Irish Constitution does not distinguish between the two; both single-winner presidential elections and multi-winner parliamentary elections are conducted under "proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote." The systems share a ballot format and an elimination engine. What changes is the number of winners and the presence of surplus transfers.

STV has been used for national elections in Ireland since 1921 and Malta since 1921. Australia has used STV for its Senate since 1949. In the United States, Cambridge, Massachusetts has used STV for its city council since 1941, and Portland, Oregon adopted what it calls "proportional ranked choice voting" for its city council in 2024. The Fair Representation Act (H.R. 4632), introduced in the 119th Congress by Representatives Beyer, Raskin, Peters, McGovern, and Khanna, would mandate Proportional RCV for multi-member House elections and IRV for Senate elections.

The adoption of "Proportional Ranked Choice Voting" as the American name for STV is a deliberate strategic choice by U.S. reform organizations. As the RCV Resource Center has described, single-winner RCV introduces voters to ranked ballots, which reduces resistance to adopting proportional systems that use the same ballot format. The terminology connects the multi-winner method to the growing familiarity Americans have with RCV from implementations in Alaska, Maine, New York City, and dozens of municipalities. This is effective advocacy, and this paper adopts the resulting term. The analytical point worth preserving is narrower: IRV -- the single-winner method most Americans associate with "ranked choice voting" -- is non-proportional, and the shared ballot format should not be mistaken for shared mechanical behavior. The difference between electing one winner and achieving proportional representation across multiple seats is real, and the balance of this paper turns on the properties of the elimination engine that both methods share.

## What Proportional STAR Does

Proportional STAR is the multi-seat form of STAR voting, the method CEMA uses for single-winner races. The ballot is identical to the single-winner one: the voter scores each candidate from zero to five stars, scoring as many or as few as they wish, and leaving a candidate unscored simply counts as zero. Nothing about the voter's task changes between a one-seat race and a seven-seat one -- the same rating gesture, the same ballot, only more names in the column.

Seats are filled in rounds. In each round, the candidate with the highest total score is elected. The ballots that supported that winner then have their weight reduced in proportion to how much they contributed to the win -- a voter who gave the elected candidate five stars has more of their ballot weight spent than a voter who gave two -- and the next seat is decided on the weight that remains. The process repeats until every seat is filled. The result is a delegation whose composition tracks the distribution of voter support across the district, produced without party lists and without closed slates. A voter who asks which member represents them has a direct answer: the one they scored highest among those elected.

The reweighting step -- the specific rule that determines how much weight each ballot loses when its candidate wins -- is the Allocated Score algorithm. The distinction matters for precision later in this paper: Proportional STAR is the *method*, the thing a voter experiences and a designer chooses; Allocated Score is the *tabulation algorithm*, the deterministic formula that delivers the proportional result behind the curtain. Where this paper makes claims about how the method behaves -- that it is monotonic, that it resists center squeeze, that it never exhausts a ballot -- those are properties of Proportional STAR. Where it refers to the weight-reduction formula itself, it names Allocated Score. The two are not interchangeable, and keeping them distinct is what lets the comparison that follows stay exact.

One feature of the design will carry most of the argument, so it is worth naming at the outset: **Proportional STAR's demand on the voter does not grow as the candidate field grows.** Scoring forty candidates is the same kind of act as scoring four -- rate the ones you have a view on, ignore the rest. There is no ordering to construct, no rank that must be left blank at a cost, no ballot that swells a new column with every entrant. That single property is the hinge on which this paper's central claim turns, because it is exactly the property the ranked ballot lacks.

## The Primary Problem in Brief

The thesis of this paper turns on a single fact about primaries, so it is worth isolating that fact from the larger case against the institution, which is made elsewhere. A ranked proportional ballot only works inside a narrow band of candidates, and holding the field inside that band requires some institution upstream of the general election to decide how many candidates reach the voter. In the American system that institution is the primary. This is not an objection to parties winnowing their own fields -- that is legitimate associational activity, and CEMA builds party endorsement into the ballot through its Joint Endorsement Lists. The objection is narrower: the state-administered partisan primary, a publicly funded election whose result gates access to November, carries real and well-documented costs -- chronically low and unrepresentative turnout, a decisive contest conducted apart from the full electorate, and the public subsidy of what the Supreme Court has recognized as a private associational act.

CEMA removes that institution from congressional elections, consolidating selection into a single Unified General Election. It can do so because Proportional STAR tolerates an open field: with no need to keep the candidate list small, there is nothing left for an upstream winnowing election to do. That is the whole of what the present argument requires. A method that depends on a pre-trimmed field depends, in the American context, on the very institution CEMA removes -- and that dependency, not the full ledger of the primary's defects, is the point that bears on the choice between proportional methods. The fuller case for why a primary-free congressional system is not only possible but preferable is made in the companion paper, *No Primaries? No Problem!*

## The Strongest Case for Proportional RCV

A position paper earns its conclusion only by engaging the best version of the opposing argument, not a caricature. Proportional RCV has genuine strengths, and several of them are real enough that CEMA adopts the same goals. (A reminder, since the terminology can blur: Proportional RCV is the American advocacy name for single transferable vote, or STV -- the same multi-winner ranked method described above. Where this section invokes the international track record, it uses STV, the name under which that record was actually compiled.)

First, Proportional RCV is candidate-centered rather than party-centered. Voters choose among individual candidates, not party lists. This makes the method compatible with nonpartisan elections -- a critical feature in the American context, where a large share of local elections are nonpartisan and where closed party-list systems are politically untenable. FairVote and RepresentWomen have correctly identified this as a decisive advantage over European-style list proportional representation for American adoption. It is a real advantage, and it is one Proportional STAR shares in full: it, too, is candidate-centered and list-free.

Second, Proportional RCV produces proportional outcomes. Any faction commanding a Droop quota's worth of support can elect a representative; in a five-seat district, a group with roughly 20% support wins about one seat. This is the core promise of proportional representation, and the method delivers it. But it is worth being precise about the source of that proportionality, because the point recurs throughout this paper: the proportionality comes from the district magnitude, not from the ranked ballot. A five-seat district makes five-fifths representation possible no matter which reasonable counting rule fills the seats. Proportional STAR, at the same magnitude, delivers a comparably proportional result. Proportionality is therefore not the dimension on which these two methods differ -- which is exactly why the comparison has to be made on the dimensions where they do.

Third, the method has a century-long track record in functioning democracies. Ireland and Malta have governed under STV for over a hundred years; the Australian Senate has used it since 1949. These states have not collapsed, have conducted peaceful transfers of power, and have maintained broadly representative parliaments. This accumulated experience is not a trivial argument -- it demonstrates operational viability at national scale, which no score-based proportional method can yet claim. This paper takes that seriously, and returns to it honestly in the discussion of tradeoffs below; it is the single strongest empirical point in Proportional RCV's favor, and it deserves to be met rather than waved away.

Fourth, ranked ballots capture real information about voter preferences. Ordering candidates is more expressive than a single categorical choice, and it lets a voter support a true first choice while naming fallbacks.

Fifth, and most important from a practical standpoint, Proportional RCV has an advocacy infrastructure no alternative proportional method can match. The Fair Representation Act is the only federal proportional representation bill in Congress, and it is not a one-off: its lead sponsor has introduced some version of it in five consecutive Congresses since 2017, a persistence that reflects genuine institutional commitment rather than a gesture. FairVote's organizational capacity, its state and municipal record with single-winner RCV, and its decade-long public-education investment have built a political pathway that no competing method possesses. The claim that Proportional RCV is the most politically achievable route to proportional representation in the United States is not wrong. It may be the strongest argument in its favor -- and this paper addresses it directly, in its own section, rather than pretending structural merit and political viability are the same question.

These are real strengths. But notice what they are strengths *of*. Candidate-centeredness is a strength of any list-free design -- Proportional STAR included. Proportional outcomes are a strength of district magnitude, which both methods share. The century-long record and the advocacy infrastructure are strengths of STV's history and FairVote's organizing -- neither of which is a property of the elimination engine that does the actual counting. Strip away what Proportional RCV shares with its alternatives and what belongs to its political circumstances rather than its mechanics, and one question remains: does the sequential-elimination engine at the method's core carry structural costs that a different proportional method avoids? That is the question the rest of this paper answers, and the answer begins with what the elimination engine demands of the field it counts.

## Why Proportional RCV Falls Short

Everything in the case against Proportional RCV follows from one mechanical fact, so it is worth stating plainly before the particulars. Proportional RCV counts by sequential elimination -- the same engine as single-winner IRV, run across multiple seats. That engine has a property that governs everything below: **it degrades as the candidate field grows.** More candidates mean more elimination rounds, more preferences a voter must produce and a ballot must hold, more rounds in which a broadly liked candidate can be cut before their support is counted. The method works well on a small field and poorly on a large one. And multi-member districts, by their nature, produce large fields -- that is what multi-member means. The method is therefore at its worst precisely where it is meant to be used.

### The Dependency at the Center

A method that only works on a small field needs something to keep the field small. This is the dependency that organizes every other criticism in this section, and it is worth being scrupulously fair about how the Fair Representation Act handles it, because the bill handles it well.

The FRA's drafters clearly saw the problem. A ranked ballot has a workable band: it needs enough candidates to make a proportional result possible, but few enough that a voter can rank them and the ballot stays legible. The bill builds to hold the field inside that band from both sides. Its default ballot, under Section 321(b)(2)(A), asks voters to rank the number of seats plus four -- nine candidates in a five-seat district -- and then Section 321(b)(2)(C) concedes that even this may not be feasible, permitting a state to cap rankings at a "maximum feasible number" no lower than five. At the same time, Section 203(b) sets a floor: in a nonpartisan-blanket-primary state, the number of candidates advancing to the general election must be at least twice the number of seats -- ten, in that same five-seat district. The bill is, in effect, drawing the walls of the workable band in statute: not too few to be proportional, not so many the ballot collapses.

This is good design, not a flaw, and it should be credited as such. The drafters were working with the ranked ballot as their given instrument, and they did about the best that can be done with it. The trouble is not the craftsmanship. The trouble is what holding the field inside that band requires.

Something has to do the winnowing -- some institution upstream of the general election that decides how many candidates reach the voter and trims the rest. The FRA's own machinery presumes that institution: Section 203 sorts out how many candidates each party may nominate (Section 203(a)) and how many a nonpartisan blanket primary must advance (Section 203(b)). In other words, the field is kept inside the workable band by the primary. The bill is permissive about whether a state holds one, but its field-management provisions only cohere if one exists. Strip the primary away and nothing guarantees the band; the ballot is free to grow until it breaks.

That is the dependency CEMA cannot accept -- not because the FRA drafted it poorly, but because the primary is the institution CEMA seeks to abolish. The harms primaries carry are not incidental: chronically low and unrepresentative turnout that lets a small slice of the electorate set the November menu; the exclusion of independents and the unaffiliated from the consequential first round; the doubled trip to the polls; the public subsidy of a private nomination contest; a standing leverage point for partisan manipulation. The voters most disadvantaged by primaries are those least likely to vote in them -- disproportionately younger, lower-income, and from racial and ethnic minorities. A reform that abolishes the primary to cure those harms cannot then adopt a counting method that quietly needs the primary to function. It would be reintroducing through the back door the institution it threw out the front.

The international record confirms that this winnowing is not optional for ranked proportional systems -- it is load-bearing. Ireland sustains STV through disciplined party nomination, with parties carefully managing how many candidates they run per constituency. That works, but it concentrates gatekeeping power in party organizations -- the opposite of the candidate-centered, voter-empowerment promise that Proportional RCV advocates make in America, and a mechanism that has no analogue in nonpartisan elections at all. Australia offers the cautionary case of what happens when the field is *not* disciplined. Its Senate, elected by STV since 1949, saw ballot papers swell until the 2013 election averaged 33.7 party columns per state -- the notorious oversized ballot, driven partly by group-voting-ticket preference harvesting. Australia reformed hard in 2016, abolishing group voting tickets, and tightened party registration in 2022; the gaming ended and the average nearly halved, to 17.2 columns by 2025. But the reforms could not abolish the underlying pressure: the 2025 Senate still fielded 330 candidates for 40 vacancies, and voters are instructed to number at least six boxes, not to rank the field -- the ballot itself encodes the concession that the full field is too large to order. The lesson is the one the FRA's drafters absorbed: a ranked proportional ballot must be actively managed to stay usable. The management is exactly what CEMA's primary-free design removes.

Proportional STAR carries no such dependency, because the property that creates it is absent. As established above, its demand on the voter does not grow with the field. A score ballot of forty candidates is the same task as a score ballot of four -- rate the ones you have a view on, leave the rest at zero. There is no ordering to construct, no rank left blank at a cost, no exhaustion when preferences run out. The ballot tolerates a large field gracefully, so no upstream winnowing is needed, so the primary can be abolished without anything left needing it. This is why CEMA's single, unified general election is possible at all: not as a convenience bolted onto the voting method, but as a direct consequence of choosing a method that does not depend on a small field. The remaining criticisms in this section are, in the end, elaborations of this one -- each is a way the elimination engine punishes the large fields that multi-member districts produce.

### Center Squeeze: The Sharpest Cost

The most consequential failure of the elimination engine is the center squeeze: a broadly preferred candidate -- one who would defeat any rival head-to-head -- is eliminated early because their support is spread across voters' second and third preferences rather than concentrated as first choices. The engine cannot see breadth; it sees only first-preference counts, and it cuts the candidate with the fewest, even if that candidate is the one most voters could live with. Over time the effect is worse than any single bad result: knowing the center is unsafe, broadly appealing candidates are discouraged from running, and the system pulls toward polarization by design.

This is IRV's signature real-world failure, and it has a name attached to it. In the 2022 Alaska special election, Nick Begich -- the candidate who would have beaten either opponent head-to-head, the Condorcet winner -- was eliminated in the first round because he held the fewest first preferences, handing the seat to an outcome a majority would have rejected in a direct contest. That is the center squeeze in a real federal election, not a textbook.

Proportional RCV inherits the squeeze because it inherits the trigger: fewest first preferences is eliminated, breadth unconsidered. Multi-seat districts soften the single-winner version -- a candidate with 20% first-preference support survives in a five-seat district where they would be cut in a one-seat race -- but the softening is partial and runs out exactly where it matters. In any round of elimination among candidates below quota, the squeeze operates at full force, and the contest for the final seat in a district collapses into a single-winner IRV race with the full single-winner vulnerability restored.

And here the evidence is sharper than "it persists" -- the multi-winner version is measurably *worse*. Marcus Ogren's 2024 study of candidate incentive distributions simulated a four-winner, twenty-candidate field and found that STV produced a more lopsided incentive structure than any single-winner method he examined. The incentive for an STV candidate to appeal to voters who oppose them was essentially nil: a candidate-incentive value of 3% at the most-opposed end of the spectrum, against 38% under STV-Minimax -- a variant identical in every respect except that it eliminates by a Condorcet rule rather than by fewest first preferences. The comparison is the point. Holding everything else fixed and changing only the elimination rule moves the incentive from 3% to 38%. The distortion is not a cost of proportional representation. It is a cost of the elimination engine specifically -- and Proportional STAR, which has no elimination engine, does not pay it. Broad support registers directly as score: a candidate rated three stars by 80% of voters outscores one rated five by a narrow 30% and zero by the rest. The method rewards the breadth the squeeze punishes.

### Ballot Exhaustion: A Failure That Grows With the Field

A ranked ballot exhausts when every candidate it ranked has been eliminated; it then exits the count and influences nothing further. This is not a rare edge case. Burnett and Kogan's 2015 study of four American IRV elections found inactive-ballot rates between 9.6% and 27.1% -- high enough to hollow out the "majority" the winner is said to hold, since that majority is computed only from ballots still active at the end, not from ballots cast.

Exhaustion is the consequence that scales most directly with field size, which makes it the clearest illustration of the dependency this section began with. A voter realistically ranks a handful of candidates; the more candidates on the ballot, the larger the share of the field any given voter leaves unranked, and the more ballots run out of preferences before the seats are filled. A five-seat district drawing twenty or thirty candidates guarantees that many voters' ballots are spent before the count ends. The FRA's drafters saw this too -- it is part of why Section 321(b)(2)(C) permits a ranking cap in the first place -- but a cap does not cure exhaustion; it simply chooses which way the ballot will be incomplete. Proportional STAR has no exhaustion mechanism at all. A score ballot expresses a view on every candidate at once; a voter who scores three at five, two at three, and leaves the rest at zero has cast a ballot that remains fully operative through every allocation round. Weight is spent as preferred candidates win, but the ballot never falls out of the count.

### The Engine's Other Flaws

Two further criticisms belong to the elimination engine but do not arise principally from field size, and honesty requires saying so plainly rather than stretching them to fit the field-size argument.

The first is non-monotonicity -- the perverse property, formally established for IRV and inherited by STV, in which raising a candidate in the rankings can cause them to lose, or lowering them can cause them to win. It is theoretically the gravest charge against the engine: a system that can respond backwards to voter preference has a coherence problem at its core. Empirically, though, it appears to be rare -- Gallagher identified plausible non-monotonicity in 20 of 1,326 Irish STV elections -- and it is not chiefly a field-size effect, though more candidates and more rounds do enlarge the opportunity. It is named here because it is real and because Proportional STAR is immune to it -- scores are summed, with no elimination order to perturb, so raising a candidate's score can only help them -- not because field size is what drives it.

The second is surplus-transfer arbitrariness, and it is genuinely intrinsic to multi-winner STV rather than to large fields. When a candidate exceeds the quota, the surplus must be redistributed, and the rule chosen for doing so -- random selection, Gregory, Meek, Warren -- can change who ultimately wins from the identical set of ballots. The FRA specifies a fractional Gregory-style transfer truncated to four decimal places, a defensible choice, but a choice nonetheless: a hidden parameter, set by drafters, that is outcome-determinative in close contests and invisible to essentially every voter. Proportional STAR's reweighting is by contrast fully determined. The Allocated Score algorithm reduces each ballot's weight in exact proportion to its contribution to the winner; the same ballots yield the same result every time, with no implementation choice left open. This is a real advantage, but it is an advantage of deterministic reweighting over surplus transfer -- a difference in tabulation design, not a consequence of field size -- and it is presented here as such.

## The Affirmative Case: Proportional STAR

The previous section showed what the elimination engine costs. This one makes the positive case, and it is not a list of the costs reversed. The properties that matter -- monotonicity, resistance to center squeeze, freedom from exhaustion and surplus-transfer arbitrariness -- have already been established where they did their work, each as the point at which a specific failure of the engine simply does not arise. Restating them as a checklist would only flatten into inventory what is better understood as a single fact seen from several sides. Proportional STAR is not five improvements bundled together. It is one method with one defining property, and the advantages are what that property looks like from different angles.

The defining property is the one named at the outset: **Proportional STAR's demand on the voter does not grow as the field grows.** Everything follows from that. Because the voter scores each candidate independently rather than ordering them, there is no elimination sequence -- so the method is monotonic, and raising a candidate's score can never cost them a seat. Because breadth of support registers directly as score rather than waiting in lower rankings to be counted, there is no center squeeze -- the consensus candidate the engine would cut is the candidate the score rewards. Because every candidate is evaluated at once rather than in a chain that can run out, there is no exhaustion -- the ballot stays in the count to the end. And because the reweighting that produces proportionality is a determined calculation rather than a transfer rule chosen among competing implementations, there is no surplus-transfer arbitrariness -- the Allocated Score algorithm yields the same result from the same ballots every time. These are not four properties. They are four consequences of scoring rather than eliminating, which is the same thing as saying the method does not buckle when the field is large.

That single property is what lets Proportional STAR do the structural work CEMA requires of it. A reform that holds a single, unwinnowed general election needs a proportional method that can stand in a crowded field without an upstream institution thinning it first. Proportional RCV cannot, for the reasons the previous section gave. Proportional STAR can, because the large field that breaks the elimination engine is, to a score ballot, merely a longer ballot.

The order in which that capacity was recognized is worth stating precisely, because it is the source of the argument's strength rather than a footnote to it. CEMA did not begin by resolving to abolish primaries and then reverse-engineer a method to make abolition possible. STAR was chosen first, on its own merits -- its resistance to vote-splitting, its expressive ballot, its consensus incentive. CEMA, in its earlier development, retained primaries; the primary question was simply unsettled, one more open problem among many. Only on returning to it did the consequence come into view: a method that identifies broadly supported winners from a field of any size removes the traditional justification for a primary altogether. CEMA's findings record the logic in exactly that direction -- the unified general election is justified because STAR performs its designed function of identifying broadly supported winners from any field size, not because primary abolition was the premise from which the method was chosen. The primary was not a target the method was built to hit. It was a bottleneck a well-chosen method turned out to dissolve.

This is an instance of a pattern that recurs across principled institutional design: a structure built from sound commitments tends to resolve problems its designers did not initially set out to address, and the fact that the benefit was discovered rather than engineered places the reform on firmer ground than any proposal that begins with the desired outcome and works backward to justify a mechanism. The single unified general election is not the assumption Proportional STAR was selected to serve. It is what a well-chosen method made available -- and that is a stronger thing to be able to say.

### The Ballot a Voter Already Knows How to Mark

There is one affirmative argument that stands on its own rather than as a consequence of the field-tolerance property, and it is among the strongest in the method's favor, so it deserves its own footing: the Proportional STAR ballot asks voters to do something most of them already do fluently.

The zero-to-five rating is among the most rehearsed quantitative judgments in modern American life. People rate drivers, restaurants, products, and businesses on five-star scales constantly, across every level of education and civic engagement, without anyone explaining what three stars means relative to five. A Proportional STAR ballot transplants that familiar gesture onto the ballot unchanged -- and uses the identical ballot for single-winner Senate races and multi-winner House races, so a voter learns one action for every congressional contest they will ever face. Now set against that the task a ranked ballot demands in a large field: place twenty candidates in strict order of preference, down to a fourteenth and fifteenth choice the voter has no real opinion about, on a grid where every additional candidate adds another row and another column of ways to spoil the ballot.

This is not a matter of convenience, and it is where the equity stakes are highest. The errors a complex ballot produces are not distributed evenly. Research on ranked ballots -- Pettigrew and Radley's multi-state analysis, Neely and McDaniel's study of San Francisco, Cormack's New York City work -- converges on a consistent finding: ballot-marking errors and incomplete rankings fall most heavily on the voters already most likely to be underrepresented, including older voters, lower-income voters, voters in non-English-dominant households, and voters of color. Overvoting, the error most likely to void a ballot outright, rises with the complexity of the grid. A harder ballot does not impose its difficulty equally; it filters hardest on the already marginalized, and the larger the field the harder it filters. Choosing the more usable ballot is therefore not a usability footnote -- it is a question of whose expressed intent survives the act of voting. The method whose ballot is the rating gesture tens of millions already perform is the method that protects those voters' voices, and it is the same property -- independence of judgment, candidate by candidate -- that makes the ballot both easy to mark and immune to the field-size collapse the elimination engine suffers. The two virtues are one virtue.

### Accepted Tradeoffs

No method escapes every flaw -- the Gibbard-Satterthwaite theorem guarantees that every reasonable system can be strategically manipulated under some conditions, and honesty requires meeting Proportional STAR's costs with the same directness applied to Proportional RCV's. There are four worth stating plainly.

**Later-no-harm failure.** Under STAR, honestly scoring a second choice can help that candidate defeat your first choice in the automatic runoff. STV satisfies later-no-harm; STAR does not. But this is not a free-standing defect -- it is the exact price of solving center squeeze, and the two cannot both be bought. A method satisfies later-no-harm only by ignoring lower preferences in early rounds, which is precisely the mechanism that produces center squeeze. One can protect the consensus candidate or guarantee later-no-harm, not both. CEMA accepts the later-no-harm failure deliberately, because the center squeeze it buys out is empirically the more damaging of the two.

**Strategic burial.** A voter can score an acceptable rival at zero to keep them out of the runoff. The incentive is real and should not be minimized, but its practical bite is limited: burial requires accurate knowledge of who actually threatens your favorite, and it backfires if the buried candidate would have beaten someone you like even less. The pivotal-voter modeling of Wolk, Quinn, and Ogren found dishonest strategies under STAR strongly disincentivized, with the residual confined to mild honest inflation that preserves the voter's true order.

**Centralized tabulation in multi-winner races.** Single-winner STAR is precinct-summable; Proportional STAR is not, because ballot weights change after each seat is filled, requiring central aggregation of individual ballot records. This is the same requirement STV imposes -- CEMA claims no tabulation advantage here, and says so. The summability advantage it does claim is for single-winner STAR in the Senate and single-seat House races.

**Reweighting legibility.** A voter whose ballot weight drops from 1.0 to 0.37 after the first round may not immediately grasp why, even though the Allocated Score formula is public, deterministic, and fully auditable. STV's fractional surplus transfers are arguably even less transparent, but that does not make reweighting self-evident. CEMA addresses this through the Electoral Science Office's public-education mandate and an auditable-tabulation requirement, but voter comprehension will take sustained institutional work, and that is a real cost.

The largest objection is not a flaw in the method but a gap in its resume: **no national track record.** Ireland has run STV since 1921; no nation has elected a parliament by Proportional STAR. This deserves transparency rather than spin -- it is the single strongest point in Proportional RCV's favor, conceded plainly. Two things bound it. First, the ballot is not novel even if the count is: the zero-to-five scale is globally ubiquitous, and STAR has run in binding elections -- the Independent Party of Oregon's 2020 statewide primary with over a million eligible participants, the Python Software Foundation's Steering Council, and, most tellingly, the Los Angeles chapter of the Democratic Socialists of America, which used both STV and Proportional STAR for its 2024 leadership elections and deliberately moved to the latter, finding it delivered the proportionality they valued while dropping the polarizing elimination rounds. A constituency predisposed toward the ranked tradition, having run both, chose the scored one. Second, and more fundamentally, the choice between methods no longer has to be settled by waiting a century to see what happens. STV was adopted into national governance in an era with no tools to predict its behavior; Proportional STAR enters a field that can characterize a method's properties -- through satisfaction-efficiency simulation, incentive-distribution analysis, and formal criterion testing -- before a single ballot is cast. Operational experience and structural soundness are both legitimate criteria. Proportional RCV leads on the first. The argument of this paper is that the second is knowable in advance, and that it points the other way.

---

## The Advocacy Landscape and Opportunity Cost

The strongest argument for Proportional RCV is not structural at all -- it is that the method can actually pass, and no competing proportional method has anything like its political infrastructure. FairVote has built, over decades of state and municipal campaigns, a capacity no rival possesses; the Fair Representation Act has congressional sponsors and a five-Congress history; and public familiarity with single-winner RCV in Alaska, Maine, and New York City gives the proportional extension a ready-made on-ramp. No equivalent legislation exists for Proportional STAR, which has never been introduced in Congress. On immediate political viability, Proportional RCV is plainly further along, and this paper does not pretend otherwise.

But political viability and structural soundness are different questions, and conflating them is its own kind of error. A proposal can be easier to pass and still be the wrong mechanism to lock into federal law for the next century. The relevant question for institutional design is not which method has the best-funded advocacy, but which method produces the best representation with the fewest structural failure modes -- and that question is not settled by counting cosponsors. The asymmetry in advocacy resources can create an impression that the matter is closed: that Proportional RCV simply *is* the proportional method, rather than *a* proportional method with specific, comparable properties. The branding reinforces the impression, because positioning the method as the natural proportional sequel to a system voters already know quietly forecloses the comparison this paper insists on making.

That foreclosure has left a real gap in the evidence. No published peer-reviewed study systematically compares STV against score-based proportional alternatives like Proportional STAR on common criteria. Ogren's 2024 work comes closest -- it includes STV and STV-Minimax in a multi-winner incentive analysis -- but it does not evaluate Proportional STAR directly. The comparative claim that Proportional RCV advocates make implicitly, that the method deserves the movement's reform energy over the structural alternatives, has never actually been tested head-to-head. The literature is largely a conversation among proponents. None of this makes Proportional RCV the wrong choice on its own. It means something narrower and more uncomfortable: the choice has been treated as settled before the comparison was ever run. This paper does not ask the reader to take its verdict on faith. It asks that the comparison be run in the first place.

---

## Conclusion

Proportional RCV is a genuine reform with genuine strengths, and this paper has tried to credit them honestly. Its advocates are right that proportional representation beats winner-take-all, right that a candidate-centered method suits American elections, and right that STV has governed democracies for a century. The Fair Representation Act is carefully drafted, and its designers saw the ranked ballot's central constraint clearly and built to respect it. None of that is in dispute.

What this paper disputes is narrower and, in the end, structural. The objections raised against Proportional RCV across these pages -- its non-monotonicity, its center squeeze, its ballot exhaustion, its surplus-transfer arbitrariness, its swelling ballot -- are not a list of separate defects to be weighed one against another. They are what a single underlying fact looks like from five angles: the elimination engine degrades as the candidate field grows. Each objection is that degradation showing up in a different place. And because multi-member districts produce large fields by their very nature, the method is weakest precisely where it is asked to work. A method that falters on a large field needs the field kept small, and keeping it small needs an institution to do the keeping. In the American system that institution is the primary -- the institution CEMA seeks to retire, because the harms it carries fall hardest on the voters least able to absorb them. A reform built to abolish the primary cannot adopt a counting method that requires it.

Proportional STAR requires no such thing, and it is worth being exact about why, because the reason is the whole argument. The method was not chosen to make primary abolition possible. It was chosen on its own merits, and its tolerance of an open field turned out to make the abolition possible -- a benefit discovered in a sound design rather than engineered toward a predetermined end. That a method selected for its expressiveness and its resistance to vote-splitting should also dissolve the primary bottleneck is not a lucky coincidence. It is the kind of thing that happens when a structure is built from principle: it solves problems its designers did not set out to solve. The single unified general election is not a feature bolted onto Proportional STAR. It is what becomes possible once the method no longer needs the field trimmed.

So the question is not whether Proportional RCV beats plurality. It does, and decisively. The question is whether it is the best proportional method available now that the alternatives can be characterized in advance rather than discovered in office -- and that question has not been seriously contested, because the comparison has been treated as closed before it was opened. This paper is the argument for opening it.
